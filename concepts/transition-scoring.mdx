---
title: "How transition scores work"
description: "Every track-to-track transition gets a score from 0 to 100 across six factors. Learn what each factor measures and how to use the results."
---

Every time two tracks sit next to each other on the board, Energy Arc calculates a **transition score** — a number from 0 to 100 that reflects how smoothly one track leads into the next. The score appears as a badge between cards and updates instantly when you drag.

## Score tiers

| Score range | Label | What it means |
|-------------|-------|---------------|
| ~80 and above | **Smooth** | A clean, natural transition. Little intervention required. |
| ~65–79 | **Workable** | Some care needed in the mix, but the transition is playable. |
| Below ~65 | **Risky** | A significant musical mismatch in one or more factors. Plan carefully or swap the track. |

<Note>
  When every transition on the board is Workable or better, the board status reads **"No ugly gear-grinds right now"** — a quick signal that the sequence is in good shape.
</Note>

## The six scoring factors

Each transition score is a weighted combination of six individual factors. Understanding each one helps you diagnose a risky transition and decide what to fix.

### BPM continuity

Measures how close the two tracks are in tempo. Energy Arc uses sub-labels to describe the gap:

| Sub-label | Meaning |
|-----------|---------|
| Tempo nearly locked | BPMs are very close — easy to beatmatch |
| Tempo shift 2 BPM | A small but noticeable difference |
| Tempo shift 3 BPM | A larger gap; may need pitching or a creative technique |

A large tempo gap hurts this factor's contribution to the overall score. Tracks within 1–2 BPM score highest here.

### Key compatibility (Camelot wheel)

Compares the two tracks' keys using the Camelot wheel, the standard system for harmonic mixing. Keys that are adjacent on the wheel blend without clashing; keys that are far apart create dissonance.

| Sub-label | Meaning |
|-----------|---------|
| Harmonically cozy | Keys are adjacent or identical on the Camelot wheel |
| Key move is manageable | A single step away — blendable with care |
| Key clash risk | Keys are far apart; blending openly will create dissonance |

<Tip>
  A key clash risk doesn't mean you can't make the transition work, but it does mean you should time the handoff carefully — swap the bassline before introducing the new key, or use a breakdown entry to mask the clash.
</Tip>

### Energy smoothness

Looks at the difference in the two tracks' Energy values (each on a 1–10 scale). A large jump — up or down — lowers this score. The score rewards gradual, deliberate energy movement over sudden shifts.

### Arc fit

Checks whether the transition supports the intended energy arc for the set. A track that spikes energy in a phase where the arc expects a plateau, or vice versa, will reduce arc fit even if BPM and key are compatible.

### Vibe continuity

Compares the Vibe tags of the two tracks (ORGANIC, MELODIC, INDIE, DARK, RAVEY). Tracks with similar or complementary vibes score well; a sharp jump between unrelated textures reduces this score.

| Sub-label | Meaning |
|-----------|---------|
| Textures feel related | Vibes are complementary or identical |
| Texture shift is sharp | A significant contrast between the two tracks' feels |

### Vocals continuity

Compares the Vocals tags (LOW, MID, HIGH) of adjacent tracks. Tracks with similar vocal presence blend more seamlessly. A jump from a heavily vocal track to an instrumental one (or vice versa) can feel jarring.

## Technique labels

Alongside the score, each transition badge suggests a mixing technique suited to the specific combination of factors. These are practical suggestions, not requirements.

| Technique | When it applies |
|-----------|-----------------|
| **Long EQ blend** | Both tracks are compatible enough to layer for an extended period — fade one out gradually while the other grows |
| **Phrase-aligned bass swap** | BPM is close but key requires care; swap the bass frequencies at a phrase boundary to control the harmonic handoff |
| **Breakdown entry or echo out** | Use a breakdown, filter sweep, or echo to mask a texture shift or key clash during the transition |
| **Short blend with filtered handoff** | Quickly blend through using a filter to smooth the handoff — useful when the window for mixing is narrow |

## Reading a transition badge

Each badge on the board shows:

1. **The score number** (0–100)
2. **The tier label** (Smooth / Workable / Risky)
3. **The suggested technique**
4. **Sub-labels** for BPM, key, and texture

Use the sub-labels on the badge to read each factor's diagnostic notes side by side.

<Warning>
  A Risky badge flags a potential problem, but it doesn't automatically mean the transition will fail. Context matters — a key clash between two tracks that never open their bass frequencies at the same time may be perfectly playable. Use the score as a prompt to think, not a veto.
</Warning>
