---
title: "What is the energy arc?"
description: "The energy arc is the emotional shape of your set — a curve that rises, peaks, and lands. Energy Arc charts it live as you build."
---

Every DJ set tells a story with energy. It starts low and patient, builds tension through the middle, explodes at the peak, then releases the floor on the way out. That shape — the rise and fall of energy over time — is the **energy arc**, and getting it right is the difference between a set that feels intentional and one that just plays tracks.

## The arc chart

The **Live Arc** chart sits at the top of the board and gives you a real-time picture of your set's energy shape.

| Line | What it shows |
|------|---------------|
| Dashed line | The ideal arc — the target energy curve Energy Arc calculated for your set |
| Solid line | Your current board order — the actual arc as your tracks are arranged right now |

As you drag cards between phases or reorder them within a phase, the solid line updates instantly. Your goal is to keep it reasonably close to the dashed line, especially through the build and peak.

<Tip>
  Keep the **Live Arc** chart visible while you drag. A solid line that dips sharply before the peak or spikes early in the Introduction phase is a sign that a track is misplaced.
</Tip>

## Why the arc matters

A set with a broken arc feels off even to listeners who can't explain why. Common problems include:

- **Peaking too early** — the energy climax happens in the Lift phase, leaving nowhere to go
- **Flat midsection** — the set never builds tension, so the peak lands without payoff
- **Hard landing** — energy drops too fast at the end, draining the floor instead of leaving it satisfied

Energy Arc flags these problems visually so you can correct them before you play, not while you're standing behind the decks.

## How the arc is calculated

Each track carries an **Energy** value on a 1–10 scale (visible in the **Track Inspector**). Energy Arc plots these values in board order and compares them against the ideal curve for a five-phase set. The ideal curve is shaped to the phase structure: low in the Introduction, climbing through Early Groove and Lift, highest at Peak, then tapering in Landing.

<Note>
  You can edit a track's Energy value in the **Track Inspector** if the auto-detected value doesn't match how the track actually feels in a mix.
</Note>

## Reading the chart while you drag

When you drag a card to a new position:

1. The solid line redraws immediately to reflect the new order.
2. Check whether the solid line moved closer to or further from the dashed line.
3. If it moved further away, consider whether a different position — or a different track — would serve the arc better.

You don't need to chase a perfect match. The dashed line is a guide, not a hard rule. A small deviation is fine; a large hump or valley usually signals a placement worth reconsidering.
